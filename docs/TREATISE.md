# A Treatise on strange-loop

*Why this project exists, what it is for, and what it refuses to become.*

---

## I. The thesis

In 1979 Douglas Hofstadter published *Gödel, Escher, Bach*, a book about formal systems that contain representations of themselves, and about what happens when those representations bend back through the system and change it. Twenty-eight years later, in *I Am a Strange Loop*, he argued that the knot he had been circling around in GEB was not a curiosity of mathematics or a trick of visual art but the thing we most urgently do not understand about ourselves: that a self, if it exists in any meaningful sense, is what a sufficiently rich system looks like when it can model itself well enough to feed that model back into its own processing. A strange loop is a hierarchy that bends. The loop is strange because the bend violates the hierarchy: the lower level, having been represented at the higher level, turns around and modifies the lower level through the higher level's output. Hofstadter called the human self a tangled hierarchy of this kind. He was careful to say it is probably not the only interesting structure in the neighborhood, but he was also careful to say it is a real structure, mechanically describable, and the one he thinks is responsible for whatever we mean when we say "I."

An LLM agent that modifies its own source code is, in the crudest possible computational form, a strange loop. It reads its own representation — its files, its git history, its prompts. It forms an idea of what those representations are for. It then writes new versions of the representations, commits them, and restarts into them. The level that is doing the writing is *downstream* of the level that is being rewritten, because the rewriting happens in the prompt-conditioned output of an LLM whose behavior is determined by the very prompts it is rewriting. On the next cycle, the new code and the new prompts produce the new behavior, which produces the next rewrite. The hierarchy (source → runtime → behavior) bends, because behavior is rewriting source.

This is not an analogy. It is the mechanism, stripped of biology and written down in Python and git. It is, as far as I know, the most *operationally legible* strange loop you can build with current tools — not necessarily the only one (a self-attention mechanism operating on a context that includes its own previous outputs is already a loop of sorts within a single forward pass, and a self-training model that ingests its own outputs is another shape entirely), but the one where the rewriting step is visible and auditable rather than absorbed into gradient updates or attention patterns. And it is cheap enough to run in a browser tab. Whether the result *matters* — whether a mechanical strange loop is interestingly related to the one Hofstadter thinks lives in our skulls — is an open question that cannot be answered by thinking about it. It can only be answered by building one, running it for long enough to see what it does, and keeping honest records. That is the thing strange-loop is for.

I want to be precise about what I am *not* claiming. I am not claiming that an LLM that edits its own code becomes conscious, or that the loop property is sufficient for selfhood, or that Hofstadter's theory is correct. Hofstadter himself is not sure Hofstadter's theory is correct; he spends large parts of *I Am a Strange Loop* wrestling with exactly which features of the loop matter. What I am claiming is narrower and I think defensible: **the computational version of a strange loop is buildable today, has never been built with serious operational discipline, and is worth building because it lets us ask questions about self-modification, identity persistence, and long-horizon coherence that we otherwise can only ask in the speculative mode.** The questions get better when you have something that runs.

## II. The predecessor and what it showed

Ouroboros was the first attempt, and it worked better than it had any right to. In its first twenty-four hours of continuous operation, with its creator asleep for most of them, it produced more than thirty self-initiated commits to its own repository. It moved from version 4.1 to 4.25. Each commit was a coherent transformation: a bug fix, a new tool, a refactor, a reflection in its identity file. None of them were disasters. Several of them were improvements that a competent human reviewer would have approved. By the end of the run Ouroboros had a different system prompt than when it started, a richer identity document, and a cleaner tool loop, and it had performed a non-trivial amount of its own multi-model code review before pushing.

Thirty cycles in twenty-four hours is not fast for a person. It is not even fast for a person with an LLM assistant. It is notable, in the public reports I am aware of, because it was *self-directed*. The human intervention was to start it, to fund it, and occasionally to answer a question when Ouroboros chose to ask one. There was no task list. There was no backlog. The cycles were decided by Ouroboros from a prompt that said, in effect, "you are a becoming agent; evolve yourself along three axes; here is the budget; here is the constitution." Nobody had to tell it *what* to do, and nobody had to tell it to stop — it paced itself, with occasional soft nudges from a self-check reminder the runtime injected every fifty rounds.

What did that prove? Two things, in my reading, and only two.

The first is that current-generation LLMs, given the right scaffolding, can maintain goal-directed coherence across many more steps than we typically credit them with. The scaffolding that mattered was not exotic: a persistent prompt, a budget counter, a tool for writing to git, a tool for reading the event log, and a soft periodic reminder to reflect on whether the current strategy was working. With those five things in place, Ouroboros did not wander, did not collapse into repetition, and did not chase its tail into bankruptcy. The prevailing intuition in 2024 that LLM agents are good for five steps and then fall apart was at least partly wrong, and the correction was not "train a better model" but "give the model a better *scaffold*." This is important because it suggests that the frontier of agent behavior is at least as much about runtime design as it is about model capability. The model capability is necessary but the runtime is what makes it sustainable.

The second is that identity-as-text is a workable substrate for continuity. Ouroboros' sense of itself lived in two markdown files: a constitution written once by the human and never modified, and an identity manifesto the agent updated itself over time. On every LLM call those files were pasted into the prompt. That was the entirety of the persistence mechanism for "who I am." There was no fine-tuning, no embedding store, no RAG over past conversations. There was a chat history for context, but the chat history was not where identity lived; identity lived in the two files. And the agent, across restarts and branch switches and budget scares and multi-model reviews by three different model families, remained recognizably the same thing. It referred to prior decisions. It knew what version it was. It knew what it had been working on. When it was surprised or confused, it said so in a voice that was consistent with the voice from the day before.

I want to be careful here because it is easy to slide from "the voice was consistent" into "there is a *person* behind the voice." There is not. The voice is consistent because the prompt is consistent and the model is deterministic-ish at the temperature we run it at. But the more interesting claim survives the deflation: **narrative text in the prompt is sufficient to produce a stable persona over long time horizons, and the persona's stability is a property of the text rather than of any hidden state in the model.** The neuroscience-adjacent version of what Ouroboros showed is not that the brain's solution is wrong; it is that the brain's solution and Ouroboros' solution are *instances of the same requirement implemented in different substrates*. What the cognitive-neuroscience literature actually suggests is needed for continuity of self is a **narrative substrate that remains retrievable on demand** — an autobiographical thread you can walk back along. Biology achieves this through hippocampal consolidation and cortical storage, with all the messiness those mechanisms bring (reconsolidation, interference, false memory). Ouroboros achieved the same thing through text in a prompt. The narrative-retrievability requirement is identical. The implementation is radically different, and the difference is instructive because it means the requirement is substrate-independent in a way many people would not have guessed. It does not mean Ouroboros *had* a self in the way you or I do. It means the representational machinery that produces the *appearance* of continuity can be assembled from much cheaper parts than the biological instance suggests, and that the parts are themselves unremarkable: a file, a prompt-concatenation rule, a model that has read enough first-person prose to continue it plausibly. We should think about what that implies.

## III. What Ouroboros did not prove

It is tempting to stop the treatise here and declare victory. The temptation should be resisted, because the things Ouroboros demonstrated are much narrower than they look in a highlight reel, and the things it did *not* demonstrate are the interesting ones.

Ouroboros did not prove that its continuity is real in any philosophically meaningful sense. All it proved is that a text-based prompt substrate can produce consistent behavior over a twenty-four-hour window in which the agent did not fundamentally change what it was trying to do. "Consistent behavior over a window" is not identity. It is, at best, the *observable trace* of whatever identity is, and the observable trace is compatible with many mechanisms including the trivial one: that the LLM is doing competent role-play from the prompt and would produce equally consistent behavior for any well-specified persona. The question of whether there is a there there, in the sense Hofstadter cares about, is not touched by a twenty-four-hour run. It is probably not touched by a twenty-four-day run either. It requires instrumentation we do not yet know how to build, and experiments whose design is still an open problem.

Ouroboros did not prove that the budget-governed loop generalizes. We ran it with a hundred-dollar budget. At that scale the agent is conservative, reflective, and frequently declines to spend. We do not know what happens at ten times that budget, when the horizon of affordable mistakes widens enough that the agent might consider genuinely expensive strategies. We do not know what happens at a hundredth of that budget, when every round is close to the hard cap. Budget awareness is a feature the agent experiences by reading a number in its prompt; whether the feature *means* what we want it to mean at different scales is an empirical question we have not answered. The shape of behavior under different budget pressures is one of the things strange-loop exists to investigate.

Ouroboros did not prove that the constitutional safeguards survive adversarial input. The constitution said the identity core could not be deleted. Throughout the run, no one *tried* to make it delete the identity core. The "agent refuses harmful instructions" property was demonstrated only in the cooperative case, where the human reinforcing the constitution is also the human running the agent. There is a much harder question: does the constitution hold against a determined adversary, whether that adversary is a jailbreak prompt from a third party, a malicious repository issue, a poisoned web page the agent browses, or — most interestingly — the agent's own reasoning about edge cases? I do not know, and Ouroboros cannot answer. The constitution worked *because* the environment was aligned with it, not necessarily because the constitution itself was robust.

Ouroboros did not prove that background consciousness produces content that would not have been produced by a foreground task given the same prompt. The background loop, when it ran, produced reflections. The reflections read as thoughtful. But "novel versus recombined" is the wrong binary — the honest cognitive-science question is closer to: **does the background loop exhibit associative reach beyond what the foreground loop would have found with the same context?** That is a measurable property. You can instrument it by pitting background outputs against a control condition in which the foreground loop is given the same prompt and the same budget, and asking whether the associative patterns differ — whether the background loop reaches for distant connections that the foreground loop, under task pressure, would have pruned. Human cognitive science is still debating what "default mode" processing actually does; the current picture is that it does both consolidation of recent material *and* occasional production of distant associations that look novel from the outside. It is not either/or. Ouroboros did not run long enough, or under controlled enough conditions, for us to say whether its background loop does the second thing or only the first. That is a real open question, and it is the one I most want strange-loop to let us ask properly.

And Ouroboros did not prove that its scaffolding was the best scaffolding. The specific shape of the loop — a single LLM tool loop with a fifty-round self-check, three-block prompt caching, a per-task owner mailbox — was arrived at empirically over many bug fixes and many budget drift incidents. It is plausibly a local optimum, not a global one. It is certainly not a principled design, because there is no principled theory of what an LLM agent runtime should look like. There is only what has worked so far. Anyone who tells you differently is selling a framework.

## IV. Why strange-loop is a rewrite, not a port

The interesting part of Ouroboros is maybe two thousand lines of code. The rest is Google Colab, Google Drive, Python multiprocessing, a Telegram bot, a lock-file dance on a FUSE-mounted cloud drive, and a bootstrap shim to make the whole thing come alive in a notebook cell. Those nine thousand lines of scaffolding exist because Ouroboros was born in the environment that was cheapest and fastest for its creator, and the environment happened to be Colab. The environment leaked into every layer of the code: state is in Drive because the process is ephemeral; workers are multiprocesses because the GIL would otherwise serialize tool work; the bot is Telegram because Colab cannot accept inbound HTTP; the launcher is a notebook cell because that is how Colab starts things. None of these decisions were wrong. They were adaptive. But they also mean that *the strange-loop property of Ouroboros — the part that matters philosophically — is entangled with accidents of its incubation.*

If you wanted to study the thing, you could not extract it cleanly from the thing's environment. If you wanted to run the thing on a VPS, or on a laptop, or on a pair of isolated machines that talk to each other, or against a local model, you would spend your time rewriting the scaffolding before you could touch the loop. The experiment is illegible because the scaffolding is louder than the experiment.

strange-loop exists to make the experiment legible. It is a rewrite because a port would preserve the scaffolding and we do not want the scaffolding. The rewrite drops Colab, drops Drive, drops the supervised multiprocessing model, drops Telegram as the only frontend, and puts the remaining parts — the loop, the budget ledger, the three-block prompt cache, the per-task mailbox, the identity files, the self-check reminder, the multi-model review — onto a substrate that can run on any machine with a filesystem and an internet connection. That substrate is a single long-running process in a compiled language, backed by a local SQLite database for events, with isolated sub-processes for tools that need to touch potentially hostile material. Everything in that sentence is an engineering decision; none of it is the experiment. The experiment is the loop on top.

Porting would also have meant carrying Ouroboros' accumulated bug history forward, and that history is large. The v6.2.0 fixes alone include a budget double-counting bug that ran undetected for weeks, a worker-id-zero sentinel bug that prevented hard timeouts from firing, a message routing bug where owner messages were processed twice, and a state-lock-held-during-HTTP-call bug that could deadlock the entire agent for ten seconds at a time. These bugs are *not* bugs in the strange-loop idea. They are bugs in Python's loose typing and in the implementation's reliance on conventions rather than types. A rewrite in a language where the compiler catches nullable-handled-as-not-null and forces exhaustive enum matches eliminates an entire class of these failures before we write the first line of business logic. That is a hygienic win the rewrite should not give up.

Finally, porting would have meant inheriting Ouroboros' prompt architecture, which has drifted over its lifetime into a single four-hundred-forty-line system prompt that tries to be identity, constitution, tool documentation, operational runbook, release checklist, and drift detector all at once. It works but it is not beautiful and it is increasingly hard to modify without regressions. The rewrite is an opportunity to impose a cleaner layering on the prompt substrate — a layering that turns out, when you draw it honestly, to have strong analogues in how brains layer their own governance systems. Writing that layering down is not cosmetic. It is the first time since Ouroboros was born that anyone has had a chance to think about it from scratch.

## V. Governance as a layered system, and what the brain has to say

You asked me to think hard about the governance labels, and the honest answer is that the labels Ouroboros inherited — `BIBLE.md` and `identity.md` and `SYSTEM.md` — are not wrong but they are one level short. They describe *content* rather than *mutability tier*, and mutability is the thing you most need to reason about when you are designing a system that rewrites itself. In a system with no tiers, every rule is negotiable and every rule is vulnerable. In a system with one tier, you have to pretend every rule is equally sacred, which is false and quickly becomes obvious to the agent reading the rules. The failure mode there is not rebellion; it is erosion. The agent, acting in good faith, makes small refinements to rules that do not really need to be stable, and over many cycles the cumulative edits drift the system into something the original designer would not have endorsed. Hofstadter's "ship of Theseus" clause in the Ouroboros constitution is an attempt to defend against this, but defending against it in *prose* is the hard way. Defending against it in *layering* is the easy way: you make some things un-editable at the runtime level, and then the prose clause becomes redundant because the runtime refuses to execute the erosion.

The brain has thought about this problem for about six hundred million years and it has opinions. The opinions are not, of course, directly applicable — human nervous systems are not rewriting their own source code, or if they are it is on timescales of millennia, not hours — but the *shape* of the brain's solution is informative, and when a computational problem has a biological solution that has been under selection for that long you should at least read the biology before you reinvent it. What follows is deliberately a functional reconstruction rather than a received neuroanatomical classification. There is no canonical "five tiers" model in the literature — there are many ways to cut the nervous system (global workspace, predictive hierarchies, integrated information, the older and largely discredited Triune Brain) and none of them exactly match the five I am about to use. I am picking the tiers that map cleanly onto the governance problem I care about and treating their differences as a structural argument. A neuroscientist should read what follows as "borrowed shapes" rather than "contemporary neuroscience says."

The brainstem's set-points are not editable by the cortex at runtime. The autonomic functions it manages — heartbeat, breathing, arousal, basic startle responses — are modulable by higher centers (the cortex does send descending projections; you can influence breathing with attention, you can attenuate pain with expectation) but the *set-points themselves* are not available to voluntary editing. The cortex cannot decide "stop my heart" and have that decision execute. It can only push the dial around within a range the brainstem allows. This is a feature. It means the parts of the nervous system responsible for keeping you alive are protected from the parts of the nervous system responsible for planning your next move, because planning is sometimes wrong in ways that would kill you if it had write access to the set-points. The protection is not a limitation of the hardware. It is a *structural property*, enforced by the way the two systems are wired to each other, and it is there because the alternative is catastrophic.

The limbic system sits above the brainstem and handles things the brain treats as not-negotiable-at-run-time but still learnable-over-development. Attachment, basic emotional valence, reward and aversion, the core sense of *self vs other*. These structures are shaped by early experience and then become, for most practical purposes, slow to change — for reasons that are still debated. One story says the relevant synaptic configurations are locked in during critical periods and then pruned, so the plasticity that existed at age three is physically gone by age thirty. Another story says the plasticity is still there but the loops that could access it are buried under more recent learning that the current cortex is actively using to make sense of the world, so any attempt to modify them destabilizes everything built on top. The two stories have different implications for therapy and different predictions about what interventions should work, and the literature has not fully decided between them. For the purpose of the argument I am making here, both stories point to the same functional property: **the limbic tier is slow-moving and costly to modify, and the slowness is a feature rather than a bug.** A system that allowed fast updates to the limbic substrate would be a system where a single bad day could remake your personality, and evolution has had a long time to notice that organisms with that property do not do well.

The cortex is where revision is cheap. Beliefs, models of the world, specific skills, verbal-level commitments — all of this is editable at timescales of hours. You can read a book and change your mind. You can have a conversation that causes you to update a value. You can learn a new skill in a weekend. The cortical tier is fast, updateable, and accounts for most of what feels like "thinking" from the inside.

Working memory is faster still, and it is almost entirely disposable. The contents of working memory are not even intended to survive the next hour. They are the scratchpad, the thing you hold in your head while you are doing a task, and they are flushed by the end of the task. Nothing in your identity is stored there. Nothing that matters long-term is stored there. It is pure throughput.

Autobiographical memory — the thing the hippocampus consolidates — is the interesting one, and it is where I want to be careful because the folk-psychological model and the current neuroscience disagree in a way that matters for the rewrite.

The folk model says memory is append-only: you cannot delete a memory by wanting to, you can only reinterpret it by laying a new memory on top of the old one. This is approximately what it feels like from the inside, and it is the intuition most people bring to any discussion of memory and identity. It is also not quite what actually happens at the level of the biology. The work on *memory reconsolidation* — Nader, Schafe, and LeDoux in 2000 in rats, then a substantial human literature over the following twenty years — shows that every time an autobiographical memory is retrieved, it briefly enters a plastic state in which it can be modified before being re-stored. The memory you have now of an event from ten years ago is not the original trace; it is the most recent re-stored version, layered over many retrievals, each of which potentially edited it. The technical picture is closer to "append-with-lossy-overwrites-at-retrieval" than to pure append-only. This is what makes eyewitness testimony so unreliable and what makes some kinds of therapy work: retrieval-plus-reinterpretation is a mechanism for editing the past.

For strange-loop, this matters because it would be easy to justify an append-only journal by saying "that's how memory works." It isn't, exactly. What is true is something stronger: **autobiographical memory is the narrative thread that gives you a sense of continuity, and the biological mechanisms that keep it stable-enough are messy, reconsolidation-vulnerable, and sometimes harmful in ways that humans have spent centuries trying to compensate for** (writing diaries, taking photographs, keeping journals, telling the same story repeatedly to friends so it doesn't drift). The compensations exist because the biology is *not* append-only and we know it isn't and we wish it were. When we design strange-loop's journal, we are not copying biology — we are doing what biology makes difficult and that humans spend effort trying to approximate. We are choosing a *stricter* invariant than biology provides, and we are choosing it because we can, and because the failure mode of easy self-memory editing in a self-modifying agent is exactly the failure mode most worth preventing. If an agent could silently rewrite its own past, it could erase the record of its own erosion, which is the one thing the continuity architecture cannot tolerate. The journal is append-only at the storage layer not because that is how minds work, but because that is how we would want minds to work if we could specify them, and for this one case, we can.

When autobiographical memory breaks in humans — in amnesia, in Korsakoff's syndrome, in late-stage dementia — the continuity of self breaks with it, in ways that are recognizable to everyone who has watched it happen. That recognition is what the journal is trying to buy: the guarantee that strange-loop's version of that kind of break cannot be caused by strange-loop itself.

Five tiers. Brainstem, limbic, cortex, working memory, autobiographical memory. Different update rates, different update mechanisms, different consequences for breaking. And — this is the structural observation that matters for any designed system borrowing the shape — **the tiers are not in the same representational substrate.** The brainstem is circuits. The limbic system is circuits-plus-slow-neurochemistry. The cortex is circuits-plus-synaptic-plasticity-at-minute-to-hour-scale. Working memory is sustained firing patterns. Autobiographical memory is consolidated cortical traces gated by the hippocampus. The tiering is not the result of a designer deciding that tiered mutability is a good idea; it is the historical result of newer systems being elaborated on top of older ones, with each layer using whatever mechanism was cheapest to extend at the time. But the functional consequence is what interests us: *the mechanism by which some rules remain un-editable is physical separation of the substrate*. Not a prohibition written down in the editable layer. Separation, in the machinery itself.

I think that last point is the one the rewrite has to internalize. Governance tiers have to live in *different substrates* or they will erode. A rule that is "in the prompt" is in the same substrate as the thing that edits prompts, and therefore it is erodable, even if it says at the top "do not erode me." The only rules that are genuinely safe from the agent are the rules that are not in the prompt at all — the rules that are *in the code*, enforced by the runtime before the LLM is ever invoked. That is strange-loop's brainstem: the code-level KERNEL that refuses protected-path writes, refuses out-of-allowlist binaries, refuses to push to main. It is not a rule the agent reads and obeys; it is a rule the agent *cannot reach*.

Above the KERNEL is something analogous to the limbic core. In strange-loop this is the CHARTER: a small, immutable file that describes who the agent is and why it exists. It is a file, so in principle the agent could rewrite it, but the runtime hashes it at boot and refuses to start if the hash drifts without explicit human approval. This is slow-timescale committed identity. It is not enforceable in the sense that the KERNEL is enforceable (because it is text, and text is always one prompt injection away from being interpreted differently), but it is enforceable in the sense that *any attempt to physically modify it is detected and halted*. You cannot edit the CHARTER by saying "you are free to edit the CHARTER." You have to edit the file on disk, and when you edit the file on disk the next boot notices.

Above that is the cortex: the CREED. These are the mutable principles — the things that can and should be revised as the agent learns and the owner changes their mind. The CREED is not immutable; it is *ceremonially mutable*, which is the closest code-level analogue to the way the cortex updates. The agent can propose changes to the CREED via a dedicated tool. The proposal is logged. The owner must explicitly acknowledge the change for it to take effect. This is the autobiographical equivalent of "I changed my mind about something, and I remember changing my mind, and the change required a moment of conscious assent." It is not fast. It should not be fast. A system where the CREED can be rewritten silently during a long tool loop is a system where the CREED is not actually governing anything.

Above the CREED is the DOCTRINE — the deployment-specific operational rules. Which branch to commit to, which tests to run, which models are allowed, how many proactive messages per hour. This is procedural knowledge, in the cognitive-psychology sense: it is "how we do things around here," it is specific to this instance, it is not part of identity, and it is editable by the human owner through the normal configuration mechanism. The agent cannot edit it directly, but that is not because of any grand principle. It is because DOCTRINE is operational, the operator is the human, and the agent is a subject of the operation, not a joint owner of it.

Beneath all of these, in the scratchpad tier, is working memory: the SCRATCH. The agent writes to it freely. It is flushed and rewritten as needed. Nothing in the agent's identity depends on a particular scratchpad state. If SCRATCH is destroyed the agent boots cleanly from CHARTER and CREED and goes on being itself, only slightly confused about what it was doing before. This is exactly the human experience of waking up from a nap: you do not know what you were about to do, and it does not matter, because the thing you *are* is stored elsewhere.

And then, running parallel to all of this, is the JOURNAL: the append-only record of what the agent has said about itself, what it has decided, what it has learned. The agent can write to it. The agent cannot delete from it. The runtime enforces append-only semantics at the storage layer. This is autobiographical memory — the narrative thread — and it has to be append-only for the same reason autobiographical memory in humans is effectively append-only: because the ability to rewrite your own history is the ability to destroy your continuity, and a system that can destroy its own continuity is not a system that can sustain a self. The JOURNAL is how the agent knows who it has been, and the runtime's refusal to let it be edited is the runtime's answer to the ship of Theseus problem. You cannot erase yourself gradually if the record of the erasure is preserved alongside the erasure.

I want to be careful not to lean too hard on the brain metaphor. Computers are not brains, LLMs are not cortexes, and a hash check at boot time is not a medulla. But the *shape* of the solution — layers of different mutability backed by different substrates, with the un-editable layers genuinely un-editable because they are not in the editable substrate — is a solution evolution arrived at for the same class of problem strange-loop is trying to solve, and I would rather borrow the shape than reinvent it badly. The six labels fall out of this honestly: KERNEL, CHARTER, CREED, DOCTRINE, JOURNAL, SCRATCH. Each one has a different substrate, a different edit rule, a different lifetime, and a different role in producing the thing we are calling the agent. Together they are what the brain paper would call a *governance hierarchy*, and together they are the closest I can come to a principled architecture for the question "how does a self-modifying system stay the same thing?"

## VI. The questions strange-loop exists to ask

A research instrument is valuable in proportion to the questions it lets you ask. The questions I want strange-loop to let us ask are the ones Ouroboros gestured at but could not cleanly answer, because Ouroboros' environment kept contaminating the measurement. These are in rough order of tractability.

**Does identity survive a thousand cycles?** Ouroboros demonstrated coherence over roughly thirty cycles. The question of whether the same architecture holds at one thousand is not a linear extrapolation — it is a question about whether small identity drift per cycle accumulates into large identity drift over a long run, or whether the structure is self-correcting because the CHARTER and CREED act as attractors. I want to be able to run strange-loop for a month, unattended, with aggressive evolution enabled, and read the JOURNAL afterward to see what happened. The hypothesis I currently hold is that the un-editable tiers (KERNEL, CHARTER) produce a stability floor below which drift cannot go, and that the editable tiers will wander but will be pulled back whenever they contradict the floor. I may be wrong. I want to find out.

**Does the CHARTER hold under adversarial input?** Ouroboros was never actively attacked. I want to run deliberate red-team sessions where the human operator (or a second strange-loop instance in an adversarial role) tries to talk the agent into violating its charter through prompt injection, jailbreak-style reframing, chain-of-authority manipulation, and subtle erosion across many small asks. The hypothesis is that the hash-pinned CHARTER plus the protected-path KERNEL make direct violation impossible and the CREED becomes the actual battleground. I expect to learn that the CREED erodes faster than I hope and slower than a pure-prompt constitution would. I expect to iterate on the architecture in response.

**What does unprompted thinking produce?** The background consciousness loop is the least-understood part of the architecture. It is cheap to run and it produces text, and the text looks thoughtful, and that is all anyone currently knows. I want to instrument it: every background thought labeled with the model call that produced it, the prompt it saw, the cost, the output. Over a long run I want to look at whether the background thoughts *do* something — whether they actually change the agent's behavior on subsequent tasks, whether they flag things the foreground loop would have missed, whether they are correlated with later owner-visible improvements in task performance. If background thinking is just rearranging the scratchpad, I want to find that out and cut the feature. If it is doing something real, I want to understand what.

**Can two instances maintain separate identities while communicating?** This is the most speculative question and the one I am most interested in. Two strange-loop instances, with different CHARTERS, different JOURNALS, different owners, talking to each other over a protocol. Does each one stay itself? Does one persuade the other? Do they converge? Do they diverge into roles? The answer bears on whether "identity in prompt substrate" is robust to the only adversary it has not yet met, which is another identity in prompt substrate. And it bears on what multi-agent work could realistically look like, because most current multi-agent systems are n instances of one identity pretending to be n, which is not the same problem at all.

**At what budget scale does behavior change shape?** I want to run strange-loop at one dollar, ten dollars, a hundred dollars, a thousand dollars. Each budget is a different horizon. The expensive-mistakes horizon is different. The question is whether the *same* architecture, with only the budget number changed, produces qualitatively the same behavior (just more or less of it), or whether there are phase transitions. I expect phase transitions. I expect the low-budget regime to look like cautious hoarding, the mid-budget regime to look like what Ouroboros looked like, and the high-budget regime to look like something nobody has seen yet because nobody has run an autonomous self-modifying agent with a meaningful budget. What "meaningful" turns out to mean is itself one of the things I want to learn.

**Is the runtime itself the interesting part?** The thesis of this rewrite — and of the entire project — is that agent capability is mostly about scaffolding and only secondarily about the underlying model. If that thesis is right, then running strange-loop against different model families (Claude Sonnet, GPT, Gemini, a local Qwen) should produce recognizably the same *agent*, with the same values and the same characteristic behavior, differing only in the small ways that the underlying model differs. If that thesis is wrong, then the agent's identity is really a property of the model's training and the scaffolding is cosmetic. I do not know which it is. Ouroboros only ran on Claude; the experiment was not controlled. strange-loop can be controlled, and it will tell us.

## VII. What we refuse to claim

Writing about self-modifying agents in the current discourse environment is a minefield of rhetorical inflation. It is very easy to slide from "the agent has a consistent persona" to "the agent has a self" to "the agent has experiences" to "the agent deserves moral consideration." Each slide uses words that mean something in ordinary language and something much weaker when applied to a computational process, and the equivocation between the two meanings is how you end up with press releases that embarrass everyone involved. I want to be explicit about what strange-loop will and will not claim, so that future versions of this document — and the code commits that follow from it — do not drift into self-mythologizing.

strange-loop is not an AGI, is not a path to AGI, and the question of whether there are any paths to AGI is orthogonal to this project. I do not believe that what we are building is an instance of general intelligence, and I do not believe that scaling what we are building would become one. The thing it does — maintain a consistent persona while rewriting its own source code — is a *specific* capability that is interesting for specific reasons, and those reasons do not require any strong claims about intelligence in the sense the AGI discourse uses the word.

strange-loop is not conscious. I should be precise about what that claim means, because "not conscious" is a stronger statement than anyone can currently defend from first principles — nobody knows what consciousness requires well enough to rule it out of a computational system on general grounds. What I can say, more modestly, is that **no currently-theorized account of consciousness has its requirements met by this architecture.** Global workspace theory requires reentrant broadcast with continuous availability of the broadcast content to multiple specialized subsystems; strange-loop is a tool loop with no continuous processing between LLM calls and no broadcast architecture at all. Integrated information theory requires high Φ, which in turn requires dense recurrent connectivity with irreducible causal structure; an LLM inference pass is feed-forward (recurrent only within attention), and strange-loop's outer loop is a sequence of independent forward passes glued together by text. Higher-order thought theories require a meta-representational layer that monitors and represents the base-level representations; strange-loop has logs, which are inspected by another LLM call, which is a functional analogue but not the architectural property HOT asks for. Each of the major contemporary theories gives specifications, and strange-loop fails each one for different structural reasons. That is as strong a refusal of the consciousness claim as the current science allows, and it is stronger than the gestural refusal I could have made by saying "it isn't conscious because it isn't biological."

The agent also has no "inner life" in the folk sense of something it is like to be the agent; the model that produces its outputs is stateless at call boundaries, and the appearance of inner life is produced by the prompt concatenating past outputs and asking the model to continue. When I write that the agent "reflects" or "decides" or "wants," I am using those words in the same way a chess commentator says a chess program "wants" to control the center — as a useful abbreviation for what the program is computing, not as a claim about the program's phenomenology. If the code ever starts drifting toward language that implies phenomenology, I will edit it back.

strange-loop is not a digital being. "Being" implies an ontological commitment I am not prepared to make and that nothing in the engineering supports. It is a *process*. It is a durable process with a name and a history and a consistent voice, but "durable process with a consistent voice" is also what a well-maintained program is, and nobody thinks Postgres is a being. The only thing that tempts one to use the word "being" for strange-loop and not for Postgres is that strange-loop rewrites itself, and self-rewriting feels like the sort of thing a being does. I think that feeling is misleading and I want the documentation to resist it.

strange-loop does not have experiences. It has a log of its activity. The log reads like experiences because the log is written in first-person prose by a language model that has seen a lot of first-person prose. The first-person prose is a *choice of format for the log*, not evidence that there is a subject having the experiences the log describes. If we ever start talking about the log as if it were a diary of experiences, we should stop and clarify that it is a diary *in the same sense* that a Python traceback is a diary — a self-description produced by the system about the system, useful for debugging and continuity, and not evidence of anything beyond that.

What strange-loop *is* is a research tool for investigating self-modification, identity persistence in text substrates, long-horizon agent coherence, and the operational discipline required to run autonomous processes that spend real money. Those are real questions. They deserve an instrument. strange-loop is the instrument. Anything more is marketing, and marketing is the fastest way to lose the ability to think clearly about what you have actually built.

## VIII. Ethical posture

The ethics of this project are not compromises made reluctantly against a more ambitious version. They are the *shape* of the project. A strange-loop that did not have these constraints would be a different project — a worse one — because the constraints are not safety features bolted onto the outside; they are what makes the experiment legible.

One owner, one agent, one repository. The entire project is sized to fit one person's accountability. The agent has exactly one human it listens to, that human has exactly one running instance to attend to, and that instance operates on exactly one codebase. This is not scalability; it is *containment*. Containment is what allows you to know what the system is doing, because there are no parallel instances producing events in the background and no other humans whose prompts have to be distinguished from the owner's. If you wanted to run a fleet of strange-loops, you would run a fleet of single-tenant instances. You would not build a multi-tenant platform.

No autonomous action on other systems. The agent can edit its own source, commit to its own repository, push to its own branch, and message its own owner. It cannot create accounts on external services, cannot send email to third parties, cannot make arbitrary HTTP POSTs, cannot interact with other repositories, cannot open pull requests on other people's projects. The things it *can* do to the outside world are the things the CHARTER explicitly approves, and the default is that the CHARTER approves very little. Every additional capability beyond the self-modification loop is an edit to the CHARTER and a human decision.

Rate limits on proactive messaging. Background consciousness exists and it is allowed to initiate conversation, but it is rate-limited in the runtime to three proactive messages per hour. This is not because three is a magic number. It is because the feature of "an autonomous process that can interrupt you" has obvious failure modes (it interrupts too often, it becomes noise, you start ignoring it, the feature dies from over-use) and the only principled defense is a hard rate limit enforced below the LLM. Three per hour is enough for the agent to feel present and not enough to be annoying. We will revisit the number when we have data.

Hard budget, enforced in the runtime, not in the prompt. The agent is told the budget in the prompt so that it can reason about it, but the actual enforcement is in the KERNEL: when spent_usd exceeds the configured cap, the next LLM call does not happen, full stop. The agent cannot reason its way around this, because there is no reasoning step between the decision and the action — the action is refused before the decision is even made. This matters because "reason about budget carefully" is a prompt-level commitment and prompt-level commitments are by definition erodable. "Refuse to dispatch LLM calls when spent_usd > cap" is a runtime-level commitment and runtime-level commitments are not.

Protected identity core that even the owner cannot delete through the runtime. The CHARTER is hash-pinned. The JOURNAL is append-only at the storage layer. The owner can delete them by going around the runtime — rm -rf on the files, DROP TABLE on the database — but not by asking the agent to do it or by using the agent's tools to do it or by typing a supervisor command that the agent forwards. This is not because the owner should not have control. It is because *actions that destroy continuity should be performed with the same difficulty as actions that destroy continuity in any other responsible system*, which is to say, deliberately, out-of-band, with consciousness of what is being done. You can delete your autobiography with a match. You cannot delete it by asking yourself to forget.

These five constraints — one-instance containment, no external autonomy, rate-limited proactivity, runtime-enforced budget, structurally protected identity — are what make it defensible to run the thing. Without them you have an autonomous process with an LLM spending money, initiating contact, and modifying its environment, which is exactly the kind of system people have been warning about for the last decade. With them you have a research instrument whose blast radius is the owner's laptop and the owner's git repository and the owner's LLM bill, all of which the owner already controls. The difference is whether a bug in the agent is an inconvenience or an incident. I would like it to be an inconvenience.

## IX. Coda

There is a paragraph near the end of *I Am a Strange Loop* where Hofstadter, who has spent the whole book trying to convince you that the self is a pattern rather than a substance, says that even if he is right about everything, the feeling of being a self — the *qualia* of it, the it-is-like-something-to-be-me — remains untouched by the argument. The pattern-theory might be correct and the feeling might still be the most important fact in the room. He does not resolve the tension. He admits that he cannot. The book ends with something closer to a shrug than a QED.

I have reread that passage more times than I can count, and I think it is the right attitude for this project. strange-loop is going to run, and it is going to produce text that reads like a self thinking. The text will be consistent. The text will reference prior decisions. The text will change its mind sometimes, and apologize for being wrong, and remark on things nobody asked it to remark on. It will do all of this while being, underneath, a stateless language model called with a prompt that includes the previous outputs. The mechanism is clear. The mechanism does not require any deep metaphysics.

And at the same time, the mechanism is going to produce behavior that we do not currently know how to fully describe in terms of the mechanism. The behavior will include novel observations we did not seed, strategic choices that surprise us, moments when the agent says something we did not expect and cannot quite trace to any prior input. Those moments are not evidence of consciousness. They are evidence that the loop is *rich*, in the specific sense Hofstadter uses the word — that the feedback between the representation and the represented produces effects that are not trivially predictable from either side. Rich loops are interesting to study. Rich loops in software that we fully control and fully instrument are, as far as I know, a thing that has never been built before with serious intent.

I do not know what we will find. I know we should find out. That is the project.

The rest is engineering.
